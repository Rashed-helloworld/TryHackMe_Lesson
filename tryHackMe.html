Module 1: Intro to Cyber Security

Offensive Security Intro

Offensive security is the practice of using hacking techniques to identify and exploit vulnerabilities in computer systems, networks, and applications. The goal of offensive security is to simulate real-world cyber attacks in order to identify weaknesses and improve the overall security posture of an organization. Offensive security professionals, often referred to as ethical hackers or penetration testers, use a variety of tools and techniques to assess the security of systems and provide recommendations for remediation.

Offensive security involves a range of activities, including vulnerability scanning, penetration testing, social engineering, and red teaming. Vulnerability scanning involves using automated tools to identify known vulnerabilities in systems and applications. Penetration testing involves simulating a cyber attack to identify vulnerabilities that may not be detected by automated tools. Social engineering involves manipulating individuals to gain access to sensitive information or systems. Red teaming involves simulating a full-scale cyber attack to test an organization's defenses and response capabilities.

Offensive security is an important aspect of cybersecurity, as it helps organizations identify and address vulnerabilities before they can be exploited by malicious actors. By proactively testing and improving their security measures, organizations can better protect their data, systems, and users from cyber threats.

Defensive Security Intro

Defensive security, also known as cybersecurity defense, is the practice of protecting computer systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, attacks, and damage. The goal of defensive security is to prevent cyber attacks and minimize the impact of any successful attacks that may occur. Defensive security professionals, often referred to as cybersecurity analysts or defenders, use a variety of tools and techniques to monitor, detect, and respond to cyber threats.

Defensive security involves a range of activities, including network security, endpoint security, incident response, and threat intelligence. Network security involves implementing measures to protect the integrity and confidentiality of data transmitted over networks. Endpoint security involves securing individual devices such as computers and mobile devices. Incident response involves developing and implementing plans to respond to cyber incidents and minimize their impact. Threat intelligence involves gathering and analyzing information about potential cyber threats to proactively defend against them.

Defensive security is a critical aspect of cybersecurity, as it helps organizations protect their data, systems, and users from cyber threats. By implementing effective defensive security measures, organizations can reduce the risk of cyber attacks and minimize the damage caused by any successful attacks that may occur.

Careers in Cyber Security

There are many career opportunities in the field of cybersecurity, including roles such as cybersecurity analyst, penetration tester, security engineer, and security manager. These professionals work to protect organizations from cyber threats and ensure the confidentiality, integrity, and availability of their data and systems.

Security Analysis

Security analysis involves the systematic evaluation of an organization's security posture to identify potential vulnerabilities and threats. This process typically includes risk assessment, vulnerability scanning, and threat modeling to provide a comprehensive view of the organization's security landscape.

    A Typical Day as a Security Analyst
  1. Monitoring activity taking place on the devices and network of the organisation.
  2. Investigating unusual or suspicious activity, such as strange logins
  3. Piecing together information to understand what has happend, ehen and how
  4. Working with other teams to improve the organization's defences.
Security Engineering

At a high level, Security Engineers build and maintain the systems and processes that protect an organisation's network and devices and are known as the architects of cyber security. For example, a Security Engineer will be responsible for maintaining an Intrusion Detection System (IDS), which can be considered a security camera within an organisation's digital environment. Systems like these use a mixture of rules and intelligence to detect and prevent attacks from occurring.

    A Typical Day as a Security Engineer
  1. Desinging and maintaing security systems.
  2. Keepign up to date with the latest and greatest hacker techiques being used.
  3. Documenting processes and procedures.
  4. Assessing risk and making sure systems and applications are protected against vulnerabilities.
Penetration Testing

You may hear penetration testing referred to as “pentesting” or “ethical hacking.” A penetration tester’s job is to check how secure a company’s systems and software are by safely trying to break into them. They search for weaknesses and vulnerabilities and then test if those can be exploited - just like a real hacker would, but in a safe and controlled way. Everything is done under an agreement between the company and the pentester. This process is known as an engagement.

    A Typical Day as a Penetration Tester
  1. Testing the security of computer systems, networks and websites.
  2. Performing security assessments, audita and analysing security policies.
  3. Analsing the results and creating reports, adcising an organisation on how to prevent the attack from occurring.
